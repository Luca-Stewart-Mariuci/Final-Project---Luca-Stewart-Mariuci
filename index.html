Schools Districts Performace and Per-Pupil Score: Does the Amount of Money Spent Increase Quality of Education

Introduction

For this research project we are looking what the impact of funding has in performance public school districts. To understand the relationship between the funding and the performance of a school district we will be looking at two variables. The Per-Pupil Price, which is the Total Current Education Cost, the total amount of money a school district spends in total decided by the total amount of students/members in a district, and the Overall Accountability Score, a comprehensive measurement of a school performance, this included both achievement: this looks at grades, performance on state tests, and resources to second-education and growth: which includes academic growth, students backgrounds in education, and impact a school has on student outcomes. This leads to my prediction that the higher Per-Pupil Price a school has, the higher the Overall accountability score will be. This information is interesting because public school funding is one a hot-buttoned issue, with many arguing that the lack of funding a school has the worse the students do. For the purposes of this research all data comes from the Wisconsin department of Public Instruction, and all is only looking at the Per-Pupil Price and Overall Accountability scores of Wisconsin school districts.

Does the average per-pupil price, that being the average amount of money a school district spends, in Wisconsin affect the average report card score school for that district. My hypothesis is that the higher a per-pupil price is, the higher the average student performance will be in said district. This is because the more money a school district spends on an individual student means that the students will have more resources available to them. This will then lead to a higher performance of students in said district. To better understand this I will be looking at two variables, the per-pupil price and the average report card score for the 2023-2024 school year. The explanatory variable will be the per-pupil price. This variable will show the average amount of money a Wisconsin public school district is spending on one student. This variable is obtained by looking at the Total Cost of Education, also known as the TEC, then dividing it by the membership of a given district. The variable of interest will be the average report card score per district. The report card score is the average performance of students in a public school district. This score is based on multiple factors including, performance in subjects, growth of students in different subjects, the ability to close the achievement gaps, and on track and post secondary readiness of students. This score is on 0.0 - 100.00 point scale, with 0.0 being the worst a school distinct can do, and 100.00 being the best. Both variables are taken from the Department of Public Instruction, which by law collects and publishes this data. An outcome that would support my hypothesis would be one that showcases the school districts that have the highest per-pupil price, and would also have the highest average report card score. There are two potential outcomes that would disprove my hypothesis. One would be one that showcases that having a higher per-pupil price would show a lower report card score. Suggesting that the schools that pay the most per student are those with a lower report card score trying to increase it. The second outcome would be one that per-pupil price doesn’t impact the average report card score. Showing there isn’t a relationship between the two variables and would imply another factor for student performance.
